Voxelab Aquila X2 → 450 nm Diode Laser Cutter

Repurpose a spare 3D printer into a capable cutter. Focus: safe power, correct PWM drive, workable firmware, and real results.

Overview

  • Donor: Voxelab Aquila X2 (Ender-3 V2 class)
  • Laser: LaserTree LT-80W-AA PRO, 450 nm, input TTL 0/3–12 V, 0–5 kHz, 12 V 3 A
  • Power: Buck set to 12.00 V, 3 A capability, aluminum shroud as supplied
  • Control: Printer PWM conditioned by a simple RC network to clean TTL
  • Firmware: Voxelab laser-conversion build for H2 board (repo now offline)
  • Status: Cutting and solid-line engraving OK; grayscale engraving pending PWM fix

Build story

“First, I took an old spare 3D printer I had laying around and began to disassemble it. It was a Voxelab Aquila X2, a cheap knockoff of an Ender-3 V2. It was all I could originally afford and it served me well as a learning bed for 3D printing until I could afford something nicer. However, now it was fated for something more exciting.

I ordered a ‘10 W’ laser from a Chinese manufacturer notorious for producing higher-than-rated output lasers and set about replacing the hot end. The module is a 450 nm visible diode, and spoiler alert: it cuts like a dream.

I installed a DC-DC buck converter to supply a reliable 3 A. The last wiring step was processing the PWM enable signal, which was trickier than expected. The printer seems to hold both PWM pins at +24 V relative to GND when ‘off’, then pull one to −24 V to simulate PWM, but does not reference board ground. Shorting to ground trips overcurrent. I had never seen PWM generated like this, but after diagnostics I built a simple RC network to invert and step the voltage down while filtering the high-frequency noise in the chip’s PWM.

With electronics sorted, I reflashed the board with firmware more appropriate to laser cutting. The board uses an obscure Chinese chip, so this was painful. After all that, I powered up with my laser safety glasses nearly taped to my face and it worked. It WORKED.

I did initial test cuts by moving unlocked steppers by hand. Cutting wood with a hand-guided laser was surreal. Then I sent G-code and cut complex patterns. A problem emerged that I’ll detail later, but overall it worked like a charm.

I cut my touchmark into wood and leather to dial speed and power. I made leather coasters with initials as gifts. Shaded engraving is not yet reliable because the board’s PWM frequency is far too low. Options: rebuild firmware for higher PWM or replace the controller. A friend offered to help rewire if I order a new board, so the plan is to pivot to a replacement soon.”

Electronics

Power path

  • Printer 24 V rail → buck → LaserTree driver at 12.00 V (~3 A).
  • Unknown buck model; stock thermal shroud retained.

PWM conditioning

  • Mainboard: Voxelab H2. Observed PWM ≈ 20 Hz, unusual ±24 V swing vs GND.
  • RC network used to invert/attenuate and filter HF noise to a clean TTL enable.
  • Dividers noted as “10 k / 20 k” with C ≈ 10 nF; final values to be verified.
  • All returns referenced to the printer board’s marked supply GND.

Docs will include a full schematic and BOM. See wiring.md and bom.csv.

Firmware

  • Laser-appropriate Voxelab conversion firmware flashed for the H2 MCU.
  • Repository has since disappeared; build notes will be recorded here.
  • Limitation: PWM base frequency too low for grayscale; dithering suffers.
  • Planned remediation: replace controller board with a higher-freq PWM output.

Results

Cutting and solid-line engraving are reliable. Photo-grade engraving pending controller change.

Safety and enclosure

  • Eyewear: Laservision USA F29.P5P18.5000, OD +8 @ 466 nm.
  • Wooden cabinet enclosure; interior black, exterior blue; external power interrupt with LED enable indicator.
  • Accurate danger labeling. Interlocks and e-stop planned with the board swap.
  • HEPA scrubber adjacent. Air-assist and dedicated exhaust are planned.
